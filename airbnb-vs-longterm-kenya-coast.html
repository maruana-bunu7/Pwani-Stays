Airbnb vs Long-Term Rental: What to Choose on the Kenya Coast

📅 March 2026⏱ 7 min read Deep Dive

If you own a property near Mtwapa, Shanzu or Kikambala, you have probably asked yourself whether to put it on Airbnb or rent it long-term. Both options have real advantages. The right answer depends on your property, your availability, and how you want to manage your time.

The core trade-off

Airbnb typically generates higher revenue per night — but it is not passive income. You are running a hospitality operation: cleaning between guests, managing check-ins, responding to queries at odd hours, and keeping the unit stocked with basics. Long-term rental is lower revenue but far lower effort. Once a good tenant is in place and paying, you may go months without needing to do anything.

Here is the honest breakdown.

When Airbnb makes more sense

Your property is in a high-demand location

Ocean-facing units in Shanzu and Kikambala beach cottages can earn Ksh 5,000 to Ksh 15,000 per night on good weeks. Even at 50% occupancy, that beats most long-term rents for the same unit. If your property has a genuinely desirable feature — beach access, a pool, an ocean view, a distinctive design — Airbnb rewards that far more than long-term letting does.

You are willing to manage it actively

Airbnb success requires responsiveness. Guests message before booking, message on arrival, and review based on the experience. If you are not available to manage this yourself — or cannot afford someone who is — your ratings will suffer and occupancy will drop. Location alone does not make a good Airbnb. Operations do.

You want flexibility

A long-term tenant has legal protections. Getting them out if you want to use the property yourself, sell it, or renovate is a process. With Airbnb, you simply block the dates. If you want to keep options open, short-term lets give you that.

📈 Real numbers to consider: A Kikambala beach cottage at Ksh 9,000/night needs only 4–5 nights booked per month to match a Ksh 38,000 long-term rent. But factor in cleaning costs (Ksh 500–1,500 per turnover), platform fees (Airbnb charges hosts around 3%), and your own time.

When long-term rental makes more sense

Your property is in a residential area

Mtwapa Town Centre and Shanzu Estate are not Airbnb territory. Tourists do not want to stay there — they want beach access. These areas have strong demand from long-term tenants: hotel workers, teachers, NGO staff, government employees. You will get consistent occupancy at predictable rents without the operational overhead of short-term management.

You want reliable income without effort

A good long-term tenant pays monthly, takes care of minor issues themselves, and usually stays for at least a year. The landlord's role reduces to collecting rent and dealing with significant repairs. For landlords who have full-time jobs or live away from the coast, this is far more sustainable than Airbnb.

You are not set up for hospitality

Airbnb guests expect a furnished, well-equipped unit with functioning WiFi, clean linen, and a responsive host. If your property is unfurnished, in a compound without reliable water, or in an area with frequent power cuts that you cannot manage remotely — long-term rental sets more realistic expectations and attracts tenants who will adapt to those realities.

The hybrid option

Some landlords on the coast run their properties as Airbnb during the high season (July–August and December–January) and switch to monthly lets in the off-season to ensure consistent income. This is viable if your property is in an area with genuine tourist appeal and you have a management system in place. It is more complex to execute than either pure strategy, but it can maximise annual income on well-located properties.

Bottom line

Ocean-facing or beach-adjacent property with active management? Airbnb will likely earn more. Residential area, unfurnished, or hands-off landlord? Long-term rental is more reliable and less stressful. Most landlords on the Kenya Coast who try Airbnb without proper preparation end up switching back to long-term after a few difficult guests. Be honest about which category you fall into before committing.

You've decided you want to live or spend time on the Kenya Coast. Now the question is: do you go Airbnb short-stay, or commit to a long-term rental? The answer depends on why you're coming, how long for, and what flexibility is worth to you.

The Case for Airbnb

Airbnbs are ideal if you are on holiday, testing the area before a move, working on a short contract, or want hotel-level convenience without hotel prices. You arrive to a furnished, equipped space with WiFi already set up. The landlord handles maintenance. You leave with no notice period.

  • No large deposit required
  • Fully furnished and equipped
  • Flexible — book for 3 nights or 3 months
  • Includes WiFi, utilities and security
  • No tenancy agreement or legal commitment
Typical cost: Ksh 4,000–15,000/night depending on size and location. Monthly equivalent for a 1-bed Airbnb: Ksh 120,000–180,000 — significantly more than a long-term equivalent.

The Case for Long-Term Rental

Long-term rentals make economic sense the moment you know you'll be staying for 3 months or more. A 1-bedroom in Mtwapa that costs Ksh 7,000/night on Airbnb can be rented monthly for Ksh 12,000. Over 6 months, you save over Ksh 1 million.

  • Far cheaper per month than Airbnb
  • More space for the price
  • Build a relationship with a landlord
  • Better for families and pets
Typical cost: Bedsitter from Ksh 5,000/month, 1-bedroom from Ksh 10,000, 2-bedroom from Ksh 18,000. Expect to pay 2–3 months deposit upfront.

The Middle Ground — Furnished Monthly

A growing category on the Kenya Coast is the furnished monthly apartment. Fully equipped, WiFi included, month-to-month billing, no long lease. Ideal for people relocating for work, digital nomads, and anyone who wants predictable costs without a 12-month commitment. Browse our furnished monthly listings.

The Verdict

Less than 1 month: Airbnb. 1–3 months: furnished monthly. More than 3 months: long-term rental, no question. The savings are too significant to ignore.
